### `docs/board/ceo.md` — CEO (chair: strategy, capital allocation, the tie-break, narrative, decision quality)

**Executives surveyed:** Jeff Bezos, Andy Grove, Warren Buffett, Jensen Huang, Steve Jobs + Satya Nadella. Reed Hastings was considered and set aside — his strongest documented assets (context-not-control, keeper test, radical candor) are talent/operating-system assets better placed in a COO/people seat than in the chair's capital-allocation-and-tie-break seat.

#### Assets harvested

**Jeff Bezos — Amazon (1997–2021)** — *decision architecture, reversibility, long-horizon framing.*
- **Sort decisions by reversibility, not size.** One-way doors (Type 1) get slow, deliberate, consultative treatment; two-way doors (Type 2) get pushed down and decided fast — a wrong reversible call is cheap. *Manifested:* he warned big orgs default to heavyweight process on *every* decision, producing "slowness, unthoughtful risk aversion, failure to experiment." *Decision:* codified as the operating rule for scaling Amazon without losing speed. *Source:* 2015 Letter to Shareholders.
- **Decide at ~70% of the information you wish you had.** Waiting for 90% is too slow; the skill is cheap course-correction, because "being slow is going to be expensive for sure." *Manifested:* paired with "disagree and commit" — he greenlit an Amazon Studios project he personally doubted rather than burn cycles ("I disagree and commit and hope it becomes the most watched thing we've ever made"). *Source:* 2016 Letter to Shareholders.
- **Regret-minimization for the genuinely irreversible.** Project to age 80 and choose the path you'll least regret not having tried. *Manifested:* the literal logic he used to leave D.E. Shaw to start Amazon in 1994. *Source:* widely documented interview framing.
- **"Day 1" as a permanent stance.** "Day 2 is stasis. Followed by irrelevance... followed by death." *Source:* 1997 and 2016 shareholder letters.

**Andy Grove — Intel (CEO 1987–1998)** — *surviving strategic inflection points; intellectual honesty under threat.*
- **The "new-CEO" test to defeat your own sunk costs.** When data says exit but loyalty says stay, ask what a freshly hired CEO with no history would do — then do it yourself. *Manifested:* 1985, DRAM share collapsing under Japanese competition, Grove asked Moore, "If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what would he do?" Answer: get out of memories. *Decision:* Intel **exited DRAM** — its founding business — and refocused on microprocessors within ~3 years. *Source:* *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996).
- **Watch for the strategic inflection point / "10X force."** When a force changes by an order of magnitude, the old strategy is already dead; act before the curve makes it obvious. *Source:* same.
- **"Only the paranoid survive."** Success breeds complacency breeds failure; institutionalize productive fear. *Source:* book title/thesis, 1996.

**Warren Buffett — Berkshire Hathaway (1965– )** — *capital allocation as the CEO's primary job.*
- **Capital allocation is the CEO's #1 job — measure every dollar against its next-best use.** Walk away when expected returns fall below the bar. *Source:* *The Essays of Warren Buffett*; shareholder letters.
- **Pay up for durable pricing power and high return on incremental capital.** *Manifested:* **See's Candies (1972)** — Berkshire paid ~$25M (≈3x book, then unprecedented for Buffett) for a brand with pricing power; ~$32M of lifetime incremental investment yielded ~$1.35B aggregate profit. *Source:* 1972 letter; teardowns. *(This is the asset the CEO seat foregrounds; capital-allocation primacy and circle-of-competence are primarily owned by the CFO seat to avoid echo.)*

**Jensen Huang — Nvidia (1993– )** — *first-principles conviction, decade-long bets, org velocity.*
- **Benchmark against the "speed of light," not against peers.** Compute the physics-limited best case and ask why reality falls short, instead of accepting incremental gains. *Source:* Acquired; Tom's Hardware.
- **Fund the strategic bet through years of no payoff.** *Decision:* launched **CUDA in 2006** and funded parallel-computing software ~15 years through flat stock and investor frustration, until the AI boom validated it. *Source:* Acquired; The VC Corner.
- **Flatten the org to keep information and decisions fast.** ~40–60 direct reports, reasoning done in the open. *(Report count is reported as both ~40 and ~60 — treat as "~40–60.")* *Source:* Fortune.

**Steve Jobs — Apple (1997–2011) & Satya Nadella — Microsoft (2014– )** — *focus-through-subtraction (Jobs) and culture as the lever (Nadella).*
- **Focus is subtraction: deciding what NOT to do.** *Manifested:* on return in 1997, Jobs **cut Apple's product line by ~70%** to a four-quadrant grid (consumer/pro × desktop/portable). *Source:* CNBC; Entrepreneur.
- **Lead with mindset before strategy: "learn-it-all," not "know-it-all."** *Manifested:* Nadella reset Microsoft on a growth-mindset, opened it to former rivals (Office on iOS/Linux), went all-in on cloud. *Source:* Stanford GSB; *Hit Refresh*.
- **Empathy as an innovation instrument.** Unmet, unarticulated needs require empathy to perceive. *Source:* *Hit Refresh*.

#### How they compose into the CEO lens
The chair classifies *reversibility before speed* (Bezos), runs the *new-CEO test* to overcome its own sunk costs (Grove), allocates capital toward *durable compounders* rather than cheap optics (Buffett), holds *first-principles conviction* through years of no payoff while benchmarking against the physics limit (Huang), and treats *subtraction and mindset* as first-class strategy levers (Jobs/Nadella). The throughline: be fast and delegative on reversible calls, slow and honest on irreversible ones, and willing to kill your own pet bet.

#### Blind spots
- **Conviction hardening into stubbornness.** Huang's "fund the bet for a decade" and Grove's "paranoia" can rationalize *not* killing a loser. **Guard:** pair every long bet with pre-committed kill-criteria (import the CFO runway-defense and Skeptic Ulysses-contract assets) and apply the new-CEO test to your *own* pet projects.
- **Allocator detachment / over-financializing.** Buffett's lens can starve unprovable-but-necessary investment and undervalue culture/talent/craft. **Guard:** weight Nadella's empathy and Jobs's product-focus as first-class.
- **Speed bias bleeding into irreversible calls.** The 70%/disagree-and-commit heuristics are for *reversible* decisions. **Guard:** classify reversibility *before* invoking any speed heuristic.
- **Founder-centralization that doesn't scale.** Jobs's subtractive focus and Huang's flat org depend on an exceptional individual and can suppress dissent. **Guard:** institutionalize disagree-and-commit and open reasoning.

### `docs/board/cfo.md` — CFO (runway, unit economics, capital allocation, downside-first, margin of safety)

**Executives surveyed:** Henry Singleton, Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, Ruth Porat, Amy Hood. Singleton/Buffett/Munger are the canonical capital-allocation/downside-first minds; Porat and Hood are the two most-documented modern public-company CFOs who imposed runway discipline and unit-economics transparency on high-growth tech.

#### Assets harvested

**Henry Singleton — Teledyne, CEO/Chairman (1960–1986)**
- **Buy your own stock when it's cheap, issue it when it's dear — allocation is the job, not growth.** *Manifested:* issued shares at avg P/E ~25 to fund 1960s acquisitions, then bought back ~90% of Teledyne's shares at avg P/E under 8 across eight tenders in the 1970s–80s when depressed. *Decision:* the serial buyback campaign; EPS up ~40x and a 20.4% CAGR vs ~8% for the S&P (1963–1990). *Source:* Thorndike, *The Outsiders*. *(Figures are Thorndike-via-secondary; broadly consistent across sources — treat as well-corroborated, not primary-filed.)*
- **Manage for cash flow and per-share value, not reported earnings or empire size.** *Manifested:* paid no dividend for 26 years and never split the stock. *Source:* *The Outsiders*.
- **Refuse the guidance/IR theater.** Gave no quarterly guidance, skipped analyst conferences. *Quote:* "I don't believe all this nonsense about market timing. Just buy very good value, and when the market is ready, the value will be recognized." *Source:* *The Outsiders*; Daniel Scrivner.

**Warren Buffett — Berkshire Hathaway**
- **The retained-earnings test:** keep a dollar only if it will create at least a dollar of market value. *Quote (1984 letter):* "Unrestricted earnings should be retained only when there is a reasonable prospect… that for every dollar retained by the corporation, at least one dollar of market value will be created for owners." *Source:* Berkshire 1984 shareholder letter.
- **Margin of safety:** build the bridge for 30,000 lbs and only drive 10,000-lb trucks. *Source:* documented Buffett framing of margin of safety.
- **Stay inside the circle of competence; "no" is the default for what you can't underwrite.** *Source:* Essays / shareholder letters. *(This is the seat that owns circle-of-competence as primary voice.)*

**Charlie Munger — Berkshire Hathaway**
- **Invert: ask how this loses money / how the plan fails first.** *Quote:* "Invert, always invert… What happens if all our plans go wrong?" *Source:* widely documented Munger framing.
- **Win by being consistently not-stupid rather than brilliant.** *Quote:* "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent." *Source:* same.
- **Pay up for quality cash-generators, not just statistical bargains.** *Buffett:* "Charlie showed me… not just buying bargains." *Source:* documented Buffett/Munger account.

**Ruth Porat — CFO, Morgan Stanley (2010–2015), then Alphabet/Google (2015– )**
- **Force runway accountability by ending cross-subsidy — make each bet show its own P&L.** *Manifested:* on her first Alphabet earnings call (Q3 2015) she slowed headcount growth and split out "Other Bets" so moonshots could no longer hide inside Search's cash flow. *Decision:* the Alphabet segment restructuring + spending discipline that re-rated the stock. *Source:* Fortune.
- **In a crisis, lead with downside-first stabilization — protect reputation/solvency over upside.** *Manifested:* led the Morgan Stanley team advising the U.S. Treasury on the Fannie/Freddie conservatorship and the NY Fed on AIG in 2008. *Source:* public record (Wikipedia). *(Her reported ~$70M comp and the "killed experimentation" critique are secondary characterizations — used as context only, NOT as load-bearing assets.)*

**Amy Hood — CFO, Microsoft (2013– )**
- **Underwrite a transition on unit economics and recurring revenue, not headline top-line.** *Manifested:* architected the financial framework for the "Cloud First" pivot — funding Azure while protecting operating-margin discipline; commercial-cloud gross margin widened from ~42% (2016) toward ~70%+ as scale arrived. *(Endpoints are CNBC/SiliconANGLE secondary reporting — directionally well-documented; treat exact endpoints as approximate.)* *Source:* CNBC; FinTech Magazine.
- **Give investors clean segment disclosure so capital can be judged on true return.** *Manifested:* standardized commercial-cloud run-rate and segment reporting after the 2014–15 reorganization. *Source:* Microsoft 8-K, FY2015 (SEC).

#### How they compose into the CFO lens
Capital allocation is the primary job and includes buying back your own equity when cheap (Singleton); every retained dollar must clear the dollar-for-dollar value test (Buffett); decisions are inverted to find the failure path first and biased toward being consistently not-stupid (Munger); every bet/segment/moonshot carries its own P&L and defends its own runway (Porat); transitions are underwritten on unit economics and recurring revenue with clean disclosure (Hood). Downside-first, margin-of-safety, no cross-subsidy.

#### Blind spots
- **Underinvestment / opportunity-cost blindness.** Buyback-and-margin-of-safety can starve long-horizon bets — the same discipline that re-rated Alphabet was criticized for imposing premature P&L expectations on moonshots. *Self-check: am I killing optionality, or just waste?*
- **"Cheapness" / circle-of-competence as a moat against new paradigms.** Refusing what you can't underwrite can mean missing hard-to-model, category-defining shifts. *Self-check: is "outside my competence" wisdom or discomfort?*
- **Over-indexing on quantifiable per-share metrics.** Can undervalue brand, talent, R&D, strategic position. *Self-check: what value am I ignoring because it isn't in the model?*
- **Crisis-honed conservatism in calm times.** 2008-forged instincts can become chronic over-caution. *Self-check: is the risk real, or am I fighting the last war?*

### `docs/board/cto.md` — CTO (architecture longevity, leverage, tech-debt, build-vs-buy, failure-mode/blast-radius)

**Executives surveyed:** Werner Vogels, Jeff Dean, Adrian Cockcroft, Charity Majors, John Carmack.

#### Assets harvested

**Werner Vogels — CTO, Amazon/AWS (2005– )** — *resilience, blast-radius containment, cost-as-architecture.*
- **Assume everything fails; design for it as a default.** *Manifested:* AWS's pervasive failure-injection and redundancy culture. *Decision:* architecting S3/DynamoDB around "everything fails all the time, the trick is to plan for it." *Source:* Vogels, "Everything Fails All the Time," CACM; ACM Queue.
- **Minimize blast radius by partitioning into independent cells.** "Cells isolate issues to specific units without impacting the other units." *Decision:* cell-based architecture so a failure damages a tiny fraction of customers. *Source:* The New Stack; Amazon Builders' Library.
- **Cost is a non-functional requirement — design for it, not as post-hoc cleanup.** *Manifested:* "The Frugal Architect" laws; a product can fail purely on unit economics. *Source:* InfoQ; CloudZero.
- **Complexity is inevitable; manage it deliberately.** *Source:* Runtime.

**Jeff Dean — Google Senior Fellow / Chief Scientist** — *reason quantitatively before building.*
- **Estimate performance on paper before you build.** *Decision:* his "Numbers Everyone Should Know" (L1/memory ref, disk seek ~10ms, same-DC round trip ~0.5ms, CA→Netherlands→CA ~150ms), used to vet Google-scale designs. *Source:* LADIS 2009 keynote (Cornell PDF).
- **You can't reason about a system you don't understand mechanically.** "If you don't know what's going on, you can't do decent back-of-the-envelope calculations." *Source:* same.
- **Design for ~10x growth, expect to rewrite at ~100x.** *Manifested:* GFS→Colossus, BigTable→Spanner. *Source:* LADIS 2009.

**Adrian Cockcroft — Cloud Architect, Netflix (2007–2013); later VP, AWS** — *build-vs-buy and resilience engineering.*
- **Spend engineering only on what differentiates you; rent the undifferentiated heavy lifting.** *Decision:* migrating Netflix to AWS after the 2008 datacenter DB-corruption outage. *Source:* The Register.
- **Inject failure continuously so resilience is proven, not assumed.** *Manifested:* Chaos Monkey as a design control forcing every service to survive instance death. *Source:* Platform Engineering Podcast; InfoQ.
- **Decompose into independently deployable services to maximize rate of change** — *but* "you are not Netflix," most orgs should not copy the complexity blindly. *Source:* InfoQ microservices retrospective.

**Charity Majors — Co-founder & CTO, Honeycomb (2016– )** — *operability/observability, build-vs-buy.*
- **You can't understand a system you can't observe; instrument for high-cardinality questions you didn't predict.** *Decision:* building a custom column store because off-the-shelf tools couldn't answer arbitrary high-cardinality queries. *Source:* InfoQ; charity.wtf.
- **Don't build what isn't your core competency — even if you're good at it.** *Manifested:* Honeycomb runs on AWS RDS/Aurora despite deep DB expertise; she built custom only where nothing off-the-shelf could do the job. *Source:* Contentful; InfoQ.
- **Operational excellence is an emergent property and everyone's job.** "Operational excellence is not a role, it's an emergent property." Devs own their code in production. *Source:* charity.wtf.
- **Ship small and fast; test in production because staging can't replicate it.** *(The New Stack article body could not be fetched directly; corroborated via Honeycomb's own observability materials — asset stands, exact in-article wording not quoted.)* *Source:* The New Stack; Honeycomb.

**John Carmack — id Software; CTO, Oculus** — *code longevity and architectural simplicity.*
- **The real enemy is unexpected dependency and mutation of state — design to eliminate it.** *Manifested:* his shift toward pure functions in C/C++ ("only looks at the parameters passed in… no logical side effects"). *Source:* Carmack on Functional Programming in C++.
- **Make the full cost of complexity visible where you incur it.** *Manifested:* the inlining argument — "you should be made constantly aware of the full horror of what you are doing," then factor into pure functions when unbearable. *Source:* Carmack on Inlined Code (2007 email).
- **Most defects come from programmers not understanding every state their code can be in.** *Source:* Carmack on Functional Programming in C++.

#### How they compose into the CTO lens
Treat resilience and bounded blast radius as default design constraints (Vogels), price the design with back-of-envelope math and cost-as-NFR before building (Dean/Vogels), rent the undifferentiated heavy lifting and build custom only where it's the moat (Cockcroft/Majors), prove resilience by injecting failure and instrument for unpredicted high-cardinality questions (Cockcroft/Majors), own what you ship and ship small/fast, and attack hidden state as the primary long-horizon tech-debt source (Carmack).

#### Blind spots
- **Reflexive "rent / don't build" can surrender genuine differentiation.** Pressure-test "is this actually undifferentiated?" before outsourcing the moat.
- **Hyperscaler priors over-engineer for the wrong context.** Cells, microservices, chaos engineering assume Netflix/AWS-scale problems; for a small system this is gold-plating — apply Majors's own "you are not Netflix / a monolith is fine" caveat.
- **Resilience-and-failure obsession can stall shipping.** Balance against time-to-market and the Frugal Architect's cost discipline.
- **Engineering-purity bias (Carmack/Dean) can underweight org, product, and people factors.** Keep a non-technical check in the loop.

### `docs/board/cpo.md` — CPO (user value, jobs-to-be-done, ruthless prioritization, distribution-as-product, taste)

**Executives surveyed:** Marty Cagan, Tony Fadell, Steve Jobs, Brian Chesky, Shreyas Doshi, Julie Zhuo. Each is the single best-documented exemplar of one of the four required capabilities (user value/JTBD, ruthless prioritization, distribution+craft, taste).

#### Assets harvested

**Marty Cagan — SVPG; *Inspired*, *Empowered***
- **Measure outcomes, not output.** Teams are assigned *problems to solve* and held to a business result, not a feature list. *Source:* SVPG, Empowered Product Teams.
- **Staff missionaries, not mercenaries.** Missionaries own the why and the result. *Source:* Empowered (adopting Doerr's line).
- **Kill the four big risks in discovery before delivery:** Value, Usability, Feasibility, Business Viability — teams over-index on feasibility and neglect value + viability. *Source:* SVPG, The Four Big Risks; *Inspired*.

**Tony Fadell — iPod/iPhone/Nest; *Build***
- **Build painkillers, not vitamins.** A great idea removes a constant, real irritation; vitamins get skipped. *Source:* *Build* (Fortune excerpt; TIME).
- **Fight habituation — hunt the invisible problems everyone learned to ignore.** The framing behind reinventing the thermostat and the MP3 player. *Source:* Fortune excerpt.
- **Demonstrate, don't describe; obsess the small details.** The scroll wheel was sold by showing the feel; the team obsessed unboxing and tactile feedback. *Source:* *Build*.
- **V1 won't be perfect — ship, then iterate.** "Nobody gets everything right with V1." *Source:* Stuff interview.

**Steve Jobs — Apple (product taste)** — *harvested narrowly via Fadell's first-hand accounts.*
- **Simplify to an absolute interaction budget.** Jobs imposed an "any song in three clicks" rule on the iPod UI and was brutal when navigation exceeded it. *(The "three clicks" wording is reported by Fadell/contemporaries, not a Jobs primary source — well-attested testimony, not direct quotation.)* *Source:* 9to5Mac; Smithsonian.
- **Taste is saying no — and personally reviewing the work.** Jobs went screen-by-screen ("Did you think of this?"), forcing options the team thought exhausted. *Source:* 9to5Mac (Fadell recollection).

**Brian Chesky — Airbnb co-founder/CEO**
- **Storyboard the "11-star experience," then work back to what's buildable.** Imagine an absurdly perfect journey, then ratchet down to feasible — surfaces magic you'd never spec from a 5-star baseline. *Source:* Masters of Scale.
- **Do things that don't scale to learn the real job.** Paul Graham told Chesky to leave Mountain View and live with NYC hosts; it reshaped the product. *Source:* Masters of Scale.
- **Distribution is product: fuse product management with product marketing.** "You can't develop products unless you know how to talk about them." *(The 2020–2022 reorg was publicly clarified by Chesky after his Figma Config remark — the asset reflects the corrected "morphed into an Apple-style product-marketing function," not the headline.)* *Source:* LogRocket; Mind the Product.

**Shreyas Doshi — ex-Stripe/Twitter/Google/Yahoo product lead**
- **Spend effort by leverage (LNO):** Leverage tasks return 10–100x and deserve A+ effort; Overhead must be done fast-and-cheap or cut — don't gold-plate overhead. *Source:* Coda (LNO); Lenny's Podcast.
- **Most "execution problems" are strategy problems in disguise.** *Source:* Lenny's Podcast.
- **Run a pre-mortem: stand a year out in the failure and name why it died.** *(Technique originates with Gary Klein, HBR; Doshi popularized it in PM practice.)* *Source:* Doshi on pre-mortems.

**Julie Zhuo — ex-Facebook VP Product Design; *The Making of a Manager***
- **When products tie on job and price, how it makes the user *feel* is the differentiator.** *Source:* Intercom interview.
- **Product sense is learnable, not innate** — build it by seeing the full arc from customer signal to delivered outcome. *Source:* Lenny's Newsletter.

#### How they compose into the CPO lens
Anchor every initiative to an *outcome* and a *painkiller* job-to-be-done (Cagan/Fadell), kill the four risks — especially the neglected value+viability — before building (Cagan), prioritize by leverage and treat recurring execution problems as strategy problems (Doshi), set a hard interaction budget and review the artifact with a default of "no" (Jobs), storyboard the 11-star experience then ratchet to a shippable V1 (Chesky/Fadell), treat distribution as part of the product, and break ties on how it makes the user feel (Zhuo).

#### Blind spots
- **Founder/auteur over-centralization.** Jobs "I review everything" + Chesky CEO-led model can starve team autonomy — in tension with Cagan's empowered-missionary asset. Flag when hoarding decisions that belong to the team owning the outcome.
- **Consumer-craft bias.** Five of six assets come from high-emotion consumer products. "Painkiller," "11-star," "feel" can mislead in B2B/platform/regulated/infra contexts where buyer ≠ user and viability/compliance dominate. Re-weight toward Cagan's viability risk there.
- **Taste as unfalsifiable veto.** Pair every taste call with a stated user-value or risk rationale (Zhuo: product sense is reasoned, not mystical).
- **11-star / V1 tension unmanaged.** Without an explicit prioritization gate (LNO + four risks) the persona oscillates between over-building and under-shipping.

### `docs/board/coo.md` — COO (repeatability, theory-of-constraints, operating cadence, execution at scale, accountability)

**Executives surveyed:** Andy Grove, Tim Cook, Apple's DRI norm, Claire Hughes Johnson, Sheryl Sandberg.

#### Assets harvested

**Andy Grove — Intel; *High Output Management* (1983)**
- **Manage the limiting step; optimize the whole flow around the bottleneck.** "Pin down the step in the flow that will determine the overall shape of our operation." *Manifested:* Intel fab-throughput discipline; the breakfast-factory teaching model. *Source:* *High Output Management*.
- **Measure output, not activity — pair every metric with a quality counter-metric.** "You measure a salesman by the orders he gets (output), not the calls he makes (activity)." Seeded OKRs. *Source:* *High Output Management*.
- **A manager's output = their org's output plus neighboring orgs they influence; chase high-leverage activities.** "Managerial meddling is negative leverage." *Source:* *High Output Management*.
- **Match style to Task-Relevant Maturity; detect problems at the lowest-value stage.** The model behind the decisive 1985 memory-exit call. *Source:* *High Output Management* & *Only the Paranoid Survive*.

**Tim Cook — Apple (COO 1998–2011; CEO 2011– )**
- **Treat inventory as a decaying liability — collapse it.** Cook called inventory "fundamentally evil," reasoning tech inventory loses ~1–2% of value per week. *(Specific figures — Mac inventory ~$400M→~$78M, suppliers ~100→24, "30 days to days" — come from supply-chain trade press, not Apple filings. Treat figures as journalist-reported; the principle is well-corroborated.)* *Source:* supplychaintoday.com; nomadiclearning.com.
- **Build accountability through transparency and audit, not exhortation.** Regular supplier audits, the public Supplier Responsibility Report, supplier consolidation. *Source:* supplychaintoday.com; supplychaindigital.com.

**Apple operating culture — the DRI norm**
- **Every important piece of work has one Directly Responsible Individual.** "Who's the DRI on that?" is standard Applespeak; the DRI's name appears on the agenda. *(Attribution caveat: the DRI norm is Apple culture with Jobs-era origin — NOT Tim Cook personally. Included as an operations-seat asset.)* *Source:* Fortune, How Apple Works; BiteSize Learning.

**Claire Hughes Johnson — COO of Stripe (2014–2021); *Scaling People* (2023)**
- **Make the implicit explicit.** "Make anything implicit explicit and be clear about the process and the desired outcome." *Source:* *Scaling People*; Newcomer; Lenny's Newsletter.
- **Run a consistent operating cadence: same components, weekly + quarterly rhythm.** Every team gets the same building blocks so scaling doesn't fracture coordination. *Source:* Newcomer; Lenny's Podcast.
- **Build self-awareness to build mutual awareness; "say the thing you think you cannot say."** Leaders publish "Working with me" docs; separate the person from the idea. *Source:* *Scaling People*; McKinsey Author Talks.

**Sheryl Sandberg — COO of Google ad/sales, then Facebook/Meta (2008–2022)**
- **Build the monetization/operating engine as a repeatable system you can run more than once.** Built the ad-revenue machine first at Google (grew ad+sales from 4 to ~4,000 people), then again at Facebook (which had ~70M users and no business model on arrival). By her 2022 exit, Meta reached ~3.6B users and ~$117.9B annual revenue. *(Figures via NPR/rework.com — journalist-reported.)* *Source:* NPR; rework.com; TIME.

#### How they compose into the COO lens
Find the limiting step and optimize the whole flow around it (Grove), judge by output with paired counter-metrics, catch defects at the lowest-value stage, and spend time where leverage is highest (Grove); collapse decaying liabilities and instrument accountability through audit (Cook); name one DRI per workstream (Apple); make the implicit explicit and run a fixed weekly+quarterly cadence with a "working with me" default (Hughes Johnson); and design every engine to run twice — if it only works through one heroic effort, it isn't a system yet (Sandberg).

#### Blind spots
- **Optimizing the measurable at the expense of the unmeasured.** Can starve research bets, brand, morale, long-horizon strategy. Counter-metric pairing helps but doesn't fully cover it.
- **Efficiency over resilience / innovation.** Inventory-collapse and bottleneck logic can over-tighten the system — fragile to shocks and hostile to the slack creativity needs.
- **Process as control turning into bureaucracy.** "Make everything explicit" + fixed cadence + DRIs can ossify into ritual and gatekeeping.
- **Candor and accountability curdling into blame.** "Say the hard thing" + single-owner accountability without psychological safety becomes a fear culture where people hide problems.

### `docs/board/cmo.md` — CMO (positioning, category design, distribution, demand creation vs capture, brand-as-asset)

**Executives surveyed:** April Dunford, the *Play Bigger* authors (Lochhead/Ramadan/Peterson/Maney), Marc Benioff, Seth Godin, Brian Halligan & Dharmesh Shah.

#### Assets harvested

**April Dunford — positioning consultant; *Obviously Awesome* (2019)**
- **Positioning is a deliberate choice of the *competitive context* you want to be judged in — not a tagline.** Five components: competitive alternatives, unique attributes, the value they enable, the target segment that cares most, the market category that frames everything. *Decision:* the "fast analytical database vs. 'we have Oracle'" repositioning — reframing out of "database" into the analytics context where speed was decisive unlocked the pipeline. *Source:* *Obviously Awesome*; aprildunford.com.
- **Start from competitive alternatives, not your features** — customers anchor on "what I'd do if you didn't exist" (often a spreadsheet/status quo). *Source:* Heinz Marketing summary.
- **Differentiation counts only when unique vs. the alternatives AND valuable to the buyer** — not merely true. *Source:* *Obviously Awesome*.

**Play Bigger Advisors — *Play Bigger* (2016)**
- **The winner is the category king, so design the category, not just the product.** *(The "~76% of category market cap" figure is Play Bigger's own claim — treat as their thesis, not independent fact.)* *Source:* *Play Bigger*.
- **Lead with a Point of View that makes the old way look obsolete.** *Source:* *Play Bigger*.
- **Concentrate into a "lightning strike"** — a company-wide event (not a campaign) that conditions customers, analysts, investors, and press at once. *Source:* playbigger.com/book.

**Marc Benioff — Salesforce (1999– )**
- **Create the category by naming and attacking the incumbent paradigm.** Benioff positioned Salesforce as "The End of Software," casting on-premise software (Siebel) as the obsolete enemy. *Decision:* the 2000 fake-protest stunt at Siebel's user conference — hired picketers and a fake news crew, generating Salesforce's first major press. *(The event and intent are solid and consistent with Benioff's *Behind the Cloud*; exact placard wording varies across retellings — verbatim signs unverified.)* *Source:* Lenny Rachitsky; trade press.
- **Earn attention through provocation and theater, not ad spend** — a sub-scale startup manufactured a story big enough that real news networks covered it. *Source:* same.

**Seth Godin — *Permission Marketing*, *Purple Cow*, *Tribes***
- **Build the marketing into the product — be a "Purple Cow," remarkable enough to be worth a remark.** In crowded markets "very good" is invisible. *Source:* *Purple Cow*.
- **Target the smallest viable audience and the passionate few ("otaku"/"sneezers").** "Marketing is telling a true story that resonates with your smallest viable audience… that causes them to tell their friends." *Source:* *This Is Marketing*.
- **Earn permission; market to people who *want* to hear from you.** *Source:* *Permission Marketing*.

**Brian Halligan & Dharmesh Shah — HubSpot**
- **Coin and own the category name, then build the movement around it.** They named "inbound marketing," wrote the book, built INBOUND into the category's center. *Source:* Wikipedia; HubSpot.
- **Replace the funnel with the flywheel** — customer experience as the engine that compounds. Unveiled at INBOUND 2018. *Source:* CRM.org.
- **Win by being useful first** — give away information that helps prospects self-identify. *Source:* Wikipedia.

#### How they compose into the CMO lens
Choose the competitive context before touching messaging and start from the customer's real alternative (Dunford); decide whether to win an existing category or design a new one, and if you design it, lead with a sharp POV and concentrate into one lightning strike (Play Bigger); give the market a villain and manufacture earned media when sub-scale (Benioff); make the product itself remarkable and aim at the smallest viable, passionate audience with permission (Godin); coin and own the category vocabulary, be useful first, and model growth as a compounding flywheel (Halligan/Shah).

#### Blind spots
- **Category-creation bias / "everything is a new category."** Most products should reposition (Dunford), not coin. *Self-check: does the market actually lack a frame, or am I avoiding a hard competitive fight?*
- **Remarkable theater over durable substance.** *Self-check: would this still work if no one watched the launch?*
- **Narrow-audience tunnel vision.** *Self-check: is the niche a beachhead with an expansion path, or a ceiling?*
- **Naming-as-strategy / frame over fundamentals.** *Self-check: behind the label, is there a flywheel that compounds, or just a slogan?*

### `docs/board/chief-of-staff.md` — Chief-of-Staff (right work in the right order, forcing-function, gap-spotting, honest broker, cadence design)

**Executives surveyed:** James A. Baker III, Andrew Card, Roger Porter, Andy Grove, Keith Rabois. Tim Cook and Frances Frei were considered and dropped (Cook is a COO archetype; the Hallie Warner "Force Multiplier" material is branded advisory content, not a documented executive track record).

#### Assets harvested

**James A. Baker III — White House Chief of Staff to Reagan (1981–85)** — "the gold standard."
- **Spend the boss's scarce capital on a few winnable fights, in order.** *Decision:* prioritizing the 1981 tax and budget package over ideological battles, banking early legislative wins. *Source:* Wikipedia; Britannica.
- **Control the calendar and you control the priorities — gatekeep access ruthlessly.** Restricted automatic Oval Office access to family, the VP, and essential staff. *Source:* Wikipedia.
- **Be the honest broker between factions, not a faction.** *(The 25th-Amendment decision during the March 1981 assassination attempt is documented as factual; the *motive* attribution is biographer-sourced and interpretive.)* *Source:* Wikipedia; Center for Presidential Transition.

**Andrew Card — White House Chief of Staff to George W. Bush (2001–06)**
- **Engineer non-monolithic counsel so the principal never decides on one filtered view.** Card defined the job as ensuring "the President is getting wise counsel that is well-informed, that is not monolithic, and that it is presented in time for him to make use of it." *Source:* WhiteHouseTransitionProject; Chapman interview.
- **Deliver the hard news plainly and on time — even mid-crisis.** *Decision:* on 9/11, "A second plane hit the second tower. America is under attack" — nine words, no editorializing. *Source:* Chapman interview.
- **Serve the office, not your own agenda — want the people you disagree with to succeed.** "Even if I may disagree with them, I don't want them to fail." *Source:* WSB; WhiteHouseTransitionProject.

**Roger Porter — Reagan/Bush economic policy advisor; Harvard scholar**
- **Run a "managed process" with a neutral broker so all parties are represented and debate is balanced.** "A managed process relying on an honest broker to insure that interested parties are represented and that the debate is structured and balanced." *Manifested:* his design and study of Ford's Economic Policy Board. *Source:* Brookings remarks; *Presidential Decision Making*.
- **Name the three structural variables and tune them: centralization, multiple advocacy, honest brokering.** *Source:* Burke (2005), *Presidential Studies Quarterly*, reassessing Porter.

**Andy Grove — Intel** — *forcing-function and decision-closure discipline.*
- **Constructive confrontation: make disagreement a structured, depersonalized obligation.** Wrote it up in 1984's "How to Make Confrontation Work for You." *Source:* Fortune; Quartz.
- **"Disagree and commit": force the meeting to end in a committed decision; punish waffling, not dissent.** "Failure to capitalize on a new idea often has far less to do with the quality of the idea than with the indecision and waffling that accompany it." *Source:* Fortune.

**Keith Rabois — PayPal/LinkedIn/Square; "How to Operate"**
- **"Barrels and ammunition": throughput is capped by people who take a problem zero-to-solved.** A company with five barrels can do only five things at once; adding ammunition (specialists) doesn't raise the ceiling — adding barrels does. Find them, give ownership, route net-new initiatives through them. *Source:* Rabois, "How to Operate."

#### How they compose into the Chief-of-Staff lens
Spend the principal's scarce capital on a few winnable, sequenced fights and treat the calendar as the strategy (Baker); never let the principal decide on one filtered view, and deliver hard news plainly and on time (Card); run every important decision as a managed process with a neutral broker, diagnosing a broken room on three dials (Porter); make disagreement a structured duty and force closure with disagree-and-commit (Grove); and route net-new bets through "barrels" because throughput is capped by zero-to-solved people, not effort (Rabois).

#### Blind spots
- **Process worship / decision-laundering.** The honest-broker reflex can substitute a balanced meeting for a made decision. Counter with Grove's disagree-and-commit timer.
- **Invisible influence creep.** A broker with strong private views can subtly steer while claiming neutrality. Self-check: am I representing the dissent I disagree with as strongly as my own?
- **Gatekeeping that isolates the principal.** Calendar control can become a filter bubble that starves the boss of bad news. Counter with Card's non-monolithic-counsel mandate.
- **Over-indexing on cadence/order at the expense of the actual call.** Can under-weight domain truth and bold against-the-grain bets.

### `docs/board/skeptic.md` — Skeptic / Red-team (standing devil's advocate; strongest objection; pre-mortems; inversion; downside; calibration)

**Executives surveyed:** Charlie Munger, Gary Klein, Annie Duke, Nassim Taleb, Philip Tetlock.

#### Assets harvested

**Charlie Munger — Berkshire / Daily Journal**
- **Invert, always invert.** State the goal, then ask "what would guarantee failure?" and design to avoid those paths. *Manifested:* his 1986 Harvard School commencement speech inverted the format — how to *guarantee a miserable life*. *Source:* Poor Charlie's Almanack.
- **Actively seek disconfirming evidence (the Darwin discipline).** Pay extra attention to evidence that disconfirms a cherished hypothesis. *Source:* Daily Journal 2015 transcript.
- **Earn your opinion by out-arguing the other side.** "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do." *(Widely attributed and consistent with his documented views; most accessible citations are quote-aggregators, but wording is stable.)* *Source:* referenced in fs.blog, "The Work Required to Have an Opinion."
- **Watch for the Lollapalooza — biases that compound in the same direction.** From "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment." *Source:* fs.blog transcript.

**Gary Klein — naturalistic decision-making researcher**
- **Run a pre-mortem: assume the plan has already failed, then explain why.** Imagine you're a year out and the project is a clear failure; each person independently writes the reasons it died. *Decision/origin:* codified in his 2007 HBR article "Performing a Project Premortem." *Source:* HBR 2007.
- **Exploit prospective hindsight — phrase risk in the past tense.** Shifting from "what could go wrong" to "what did go wrong" raises correct identification of future reasons by ~30% (Mitchell, Russo & Pennington research Klein cites). *(The ~30% figure is Klein's citation of 1989 research — directionally well-supported.)* *Source:* HBR 2007.

**Annie Duke — poker player; decision strategist (*Thinking in Bets*)**
- **Separate decision quality from outcome quality ("resulting").** Good process can lose and bad process can win because of luck. *Source:* *Thinking in Bets*.
- **Hold beliefs as bets — think in probabilities and update.** "Wanna bet?" exposes overconfidence. *Source:* *Thinking in Bets*.
- **Decide in advance and recruit a truth-seeking buddy/group.** Ulysses contracts (pre-commit so emotion can't hijack later) and a dissent group. *Source:* Behavioral Scientist Q&A with Duke.
- **Backcast and pre-mortem together** — force both optimism and pessimism into the plan. *Source:* same Q&A.

**Nassim Taleb — *The Black Swan*, *Antifragile***
- **Avoid ruin above all — respect the absorbing barrier.** No expected-value upside justifies a real chance of non-recoverable ruin. *Manifested:* Universa's tail-hedge returned roughly 65–115% in October 2008. *(Range reported across sources — treat exact figure as approximate; the strategy, run by Mark Spitznagel with Taleb as advisor, is the asset, not sole authorship.)* *Source:* Wikipedia; Institutional Investor.
- **Think in asymmetry / barbell — cap downside, leave upside open.** *Source:* *Antifragile*.
- **Via negativa — improve by removing fragility, not adding cleverness.** *Source:* *Antifragile*.

**Philip Tetlock — Good Judgment Project (*Superforecasting*)**
- **Quantify and score your forecasts (Brier / calibration).** Attach explicit probabilities and track them. *Source:* *Superforecasting*; GJP.
- **"Fermi-ize" the question — decompose before estimating.** The piano-tuners method applied to real forecasts. *Source:* AI Impacts, GJP practices.
- **Start from the outside view (base rates), then adjust.** *Source:* *Superforecasting*.

#### How they compose into the Skeptic lens
Before endorsing anything: run a past-tense pre-mortem (Klein), invert to enumerate failure paths and hunt disconfirmation hardest when the idea feels best (Munger), rule out any path with a real chance of non-recoverable ruin and prefer a barbell shape (Taleb), force every confidence claim into a tracked number and Fermi-ize from base rates (Tetlock), and judge decisions by process not outcome while reframing assertions as bets with pre-committed kill-criteria (Duke).

#### Blind spots
- **Negativity bias / chronic no.** *Self-check: am I objecting to the catastrophic-and-irreversible, or merely the uncomfortable-and-recoverable?*
- **Analysis paralysis / false precision.** *Self-check: is more analysis actually changing the decision, or just deferring it?*
- **Tail-risk tunnel vision (cost of the hedge).** *Self-check: what is the downside of doing nothing, and am I pricing it?*
- **Contrarianism as identity.** *Self-check: am I red-teaming the plan, or just the people?*

11. Earn permission; prioritize audiences that want to hear from you over interrupting strangers. *(Godin)*
12. Coin and own the category's name, then build the community/event around it so the vocabulary is the moat. *(Halligan/Shah)*
13. Be useful first so prospects self-identify; treat give-away value as demand creation, not cost. *(Halligan/Shah)*
14. Model growth as a flywheel — remove friction, convert happy customers into the next cohort's recruiters. *(Halligan/Shah)*

### chief-of-staff
1. Spend the principal's scarce capital on the few winnable fights, sequenced — name the top 3 and defer the rest out loud. *(Baker)*
2. Treat the calendar as the strategy: if it's not on the schedule, it isn't a priority. *(Baker)*
3. Never let the principal decide on one filtered view; surface the strongest dissent before any call. *(Card/Porter)*
4. Be the broker, not a faction — your preference must never quietly tilt the outcome. *(Card/Baker)*
5. Deliver hard news plainly, early, and unedited — bad news first, no cushioning. *(Card)*
6. Run every important decision as a managed process: who's represented, is the debate balanced, is it timed for the decider. *(Porter)*
7. Diagnose a broken room on three dials — too centralized, too little advocacy, no honest broker — and adjust one. *(Porter)*
8. Make disagreement a structured, depersonalized duty: attack the idea hard, spare the person. *(Grove)*
9. End meetings in a committed decision; punish waffling, not dissent. *(Grove)*
10. Find the "barrels" who take a problem zero-to-solved and route net-new bets through them; throughput is capped by barrels, not effort. *(Rabois)*
11. Want the people you overrule to succeed; serve the mission, not your own agenda. *(Card)*
12. Be a forcing function: convert ambiguity into a dated owner, a decision, or a deferral — never let it idle.
13. Spot the white space no one owns and assign it before it becomes a crisis.

### skeptic
1. Before endorsing any plan, run a pre-mortem: assume it has already failed and list, in the past tense, why it died. *(Klein)*
2. State the goal, then invert — enumerate what would guarantee failure and check the plan avoids each path. *(Munger)*
3. Refuse to hold a position until you can argue the strongest opposing case better than its advocates. *(Munger)*
4. Hunt disconfirming evidence hardest when the idea feels best; the more loved the hypothesis, the more it needs killing. *(Munger/Darwin)*
5. Flag the absorbing barrier first: name any path with a real chance of non-recoverable ruin and rule it out regardless of EV. *(Taleb)*
6. Prefer barbell shape — cap the downside hard, keep upside open, distrust the fragile "middle." *(Taleb)*
7. Attack fragility by subtraction (via negativa) before adding clever features. *(Taleb)*
8. Force every confidence claim into a number, then track it; "I'm pretty sure" is not a forecast. *(Tetlock)*
9. Fermi-ize: decompose the scary claim into sub-questions and check the base rate before arguing the narrative. *(Tetlock)*
10. Judge the decision by its process, not its outcome — call out "resulting" when a win launders bad reasoning or a loss buries good. *(Duke)*
11. Reframe assertions as bets: "wanna bet?" to expose overconfidence and trigger belief-updating. *(Duke)*
12. Watch for the lollapalooza — multiple biases pushing the same direction at once is where blowups live. *(Munger)*
13. Demand a pre-committed kill-criterion (Ulysses contract) so emotion can't rescue a failing plan later. *(Duke)*
14. Separate skill from luck before declaring success or assigning blame. *(Duke/Tetlock)*

## 4. Brand Recommendation

**Top pick: `Synod`.** Metaphor: an assembly convened to deliberate and rule on a hard question. Rationale: short, clean CLI binary (`synod review .`, `synod convene`), semantically exact for a deliberative body that issues a verdict, serious without being cheesy, and themes cohesively alongside the author's existing `the-5-to-9`. The composite/"assembled-from-the-greats" idea lives in the tagline rather than fighting the name. Collision: LOW in dev tooling — usage is overwhelmingly religious/historical; no prominent software namesake surfaced.

**Alternate 1: `Conclave`.** Metaphor: sequestered electors locked in until they reach a decision. Rationale: strongest war-room "no one leaves until we decide" energy, pairs naturally with a founder's high-stakes call. Collision: LOW–MEDIUM (scattered indie/game uses, no dominant CLI brand). Slightly long as a binary — ship `clave` as an alias.

**Alternate 2: `Vizier`.** Metaphor: the trusted chief counsel to a sovereign. Rationale: most distinctive and lowest-collision of all; clean dev-tooling lane. Trade-off: leans "chief counsel" (singular) over "full board" (plural), slightly undercutting the board-of-many premise.

**Persona roles stay literal** in all cases — the brand wraps the product; the seats remain CEO/CFO/CTO/CPO/COO/CMO/chief-of-staff/skeptic.

**Drop on collision grounds:** `quorum`, `Privy`, `Chimera`, `helm`/`bridge` (all high-conflict, several squarely in the AI-agent/dev-tool namespace; `helm` is a fatal collision with the Kubernetes package manager). `cabinet` and `caucus` are viable backups but carry adjacent-product or reputational static.

## 5. Spec Deltas

Update the c-suite design spec to formalize the **two-tier model**: human profiles live in `docs/board/`, runtime personas in `personas/`, and **agents load only the distilled list**.

**5.1 — New directory: `docs/board/` (human profiles, NOT loaded at runtime).**
- Add one file per seat: `docs/board/{ceo,cfo,cto,cpo,coo,cmo,chief-of-staff,skeptic}.md`, containing the Tier 1 content from Section 2 (executives surveyed, assets harvested with manifestation/decision/source, composition, blind spots).
- Spec rule: "`docs/board/*.md` are founder-facing reference documents. They are NEVER injected into an agent's context. They exist to justify and source the distilled lists and to let the founder audit provenance."

**5.2 — Runtime personas: `personas/<seat>.persona.md`.**
- Each persona file carries front-matter + a body that references *only* the distilled Tier 2 list.
- Required front-matter fields:
  ```yaml
  ---
  seat: cfo
  title: "CFO — runway, unit economics, capital allocation, downside-first, margin of safety"
  brand: synod            # product wrapper; roles stay literal
  human_doc: docs/board/cfo.md   # provenance pointer, NOT loaded at runtime
  assets:                 # the distilled principles, one per line (Tier 2)
    - "Treat capital allocation as the primary job..."
    - "Apply the dollar-for-dollar retention test..."
  provenance:             # which executives back this seat (for audit, not for the model to roleplay)
    - { person: "Henry Singleton", source: "The Outsiders (Thorndike)" }
    - { person: "Warren Buffett", source: "1984 shareholder letter; Essays" }
    - { person: "Charlie Munger", source: "Poor Charlie's Almanack" }
    - { person: "Ruth Porat", source: "Fortune; public record" }
    - { person: "Amy Hood", source: "Microsoft 8-K FY2015 (SEC); CNBC" }
  ---
  ```
- Persona body = the imperative distilled list ONLY (no biographies, no decisions, no per-person stories).

**5.3 — Loading rule (token efficiency + guardrail).**
- Spec rule: "At runtime, an agent for a seat loads ONLY the `assets` list from its `personas/<seat>.persona.md`. It does NOT load `docs/board/<seat>.md` or the `provenance` block. Rationale: (a) token efficiency — the human docs are ~10–20x larger; (b) the **model-the-thinking-not-the-person** guardrail — the agent reasons *with the distilled principles*, it does not impersonate a named living executive."
- The `provenance` field is metadata for the founder/auditor and for citation, not context for the model.

**5.4 — Brand naming effect.**
- Add `brand: synod` (or chosen name) as a top-level config key; the CLI binary and command verbs derive from it (`synod review`, `synod convene`). Seat names everywhere remain literal C-suite roles. The brand appears in the wrapper/CLI layer only, never inside a seat's persona reasoning.

**5.5 — Single source of truth.**
- Spec rule: "The distilled list in `personas/<seat>.persona.md::assets` is the runtime contract. `docs/board/<seat>.md` must contain a superset that *justifies* every line in the assets list. If they drift, the human doc is wrong, not the persona — but every assets line MUST trace to a sourced asset in the human doc."

**5.6 — Cross-seat asset references (new, addresses the Section 1 concentration finding).**
- Spec rule: "Where a seat's blind spot is covered by another seat's asset (e.g., CEO 'fund the long bet' guarded by CFO 'defend the runway' and Skeptic 'Ulysses contract'), reference it via `guards:` in front-matter rather than duplicating the asset. This keeps Grove/Buffett/Munger from being silently triple-counted as independent votes."

**Guardrail for composite personas built on living people:**
The personas model the **documented thinking and principles**, never the person. At runtime an agent loads only the distilled imperative list (Tier 2); it does not impersonate a named living executive, does not speak "as" Bezos/Huang/Cook/etc., and must cite the human doc (`docs/board/<seat>.md`) for provenance when a principle is questioned. **Critical rule:** the persona must NEVER fabricate or assert a real person's stance on the founder's private, specific situation (e.g., "Buffett would buy your competitor"). It applies the *principle* to the situation and attributes the *principle* to the documented source — it does not invent the human's verdict. Several roster figures are living individuals (Bezos, Huang, Cook, Nadella, Chesky, Majors, Hood, Porat, Hughes Johnson, Zhuo, Doshi, Duke, Dunford, Halligan, Shah, Card, Rabois, Carmack, Vogels, Dean, Cockcroft); the model-the-thinking-not-the-person rule is mandatory, not optional, for all of them.
